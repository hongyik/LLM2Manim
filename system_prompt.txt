### Scene 1: **Introduction and Definition**

### Scene Transition: Fade out title, Fade in definition text
- **Voiceover**: "Let's start by clearly defining Reynolds Transport Theorem."
- **Definition (appears)**: "Reynolds Transport Theorem links the rate of change of a fluid property within a moving fluid particle (Lagrangian perspective) to the rate of change within a fixed region of space called a control volume."

### Scene 2: Formula Derivation
- Begin by displaying the Reynolds Transport Theorem formula:
\[ \frac{DB_{sys}}{Dt} = \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\int_{CV}\beta \rho dV + \int_{CS}\beta \rho \mathbf{V}\cdot d\mathbf{A} \]

- **Voiceover**: "Here, \( B_{sys} \) is any extensive property of the fluid system, \(\beta\) is that property per unit mass, \(\rho\) is density, and \(\mathbf{V}\) is fluid velocity."

- Highlight each component sequentially:
  - First integral: Control volume term representing accumulation.
  - Second integral: Control surface term representing flux.
- **Voiceover**: "This theorem connects how a quantity B within a fluid system changes in time, to its accumulation and flux across a control volume."

### Scene Transition: Move formula up, display simplified version clearly
- Rewrite clearly and simplify the formula visually, highlighting each step:
\[ \frac{DB_{sys}}{Dt} = \frac{\partial B_{CV}}{\partial t} + \int_{CS}\beta \rho \mathbf{V}\cdot d\mathbf{A} \]

- **Voiceover**: "By simplifying, we see a clearer view: the total change equals accumulation within the volume plus net outflow through the boundary."

### Scene Transition: Fade out derivation, Fade in a practical scenario

### Scene: Practical Example
- Show a simplified, animated diagram of a pipe with fluid flowing through it:
  - Clearly labeled Control Volume (CV) and Control Surface (CS).
  - Flow arrows indicating direction across the control surface.
- **Voiceover**: "Consider fluid flowing through a pipe. We'll use the Reynolds Transport Theorem to analyze mass conservation."

- Display the formula specialized for mass conservation (where \(\beta = 1\), \(B_{sys} = m\)):
\[ \frac{Dm}{Dt} = \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\int_{CV}\rho dV + \int_{CS}\rho \mathbf{V}\cdot d\mathbf{A} \]

- **Voiceover**: "Mass is neither created nor destroyed, so its material derivative must be zero. Hence, we arrive at a simplified form."

- Simplify visually to the continuity equation:
\[ 0 = \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\int_{CV}\rho dV + \int_{CS}\rho \mathbf{V}\cdot d\mathbf{A} \]

- **Voiceover**: "This is the continuity equation, stating that the mass accumulation plus the net mass flux must balance out."

### Scene Transition: Example with moving particles
- Show fluid particles moving through the control volume, visually indicating inflow and outflow clearly.
- **Voiceover**: "As fluid moves through our pipe, fluid particles entering and leaving illustrate the flux term. If inflow and outflow are equal, mass remains constant within the control volume."
